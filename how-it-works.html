How PKI-Based Ticketing Works

Understanding the cryptographic technology that secures your tickets

The SecureTix Process

Our system uses Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) to ensure every ticket is authentic, tamper-proof, and verifiable.

1

Key Pair Generation

When you register, SecureTix generates a unique RSA-2048 key pair for your account:

Private Key

Encrypted with your PIN and stored securely in your browser

Public Key

Shared with others to verify your signatures

2

Certificate Issuance

Our Certificate Authority (CA) issues you a digital certificate (X.509 standard) that:

  • Binds your identity to your public key
  • Is cryptographically signed by the CA
  • Contains validity period and usage constraints
  • Can be verified by anyone against our CA
Certificate Contents: Subject Name, Public Key, Serial Number, Issuer (CA), Validity Period, Digital Signature
3

Ticket Signing

When you purchase a ticket:

  1. Ticket data is created with event details and your information
  2. A cryptographic hash (SHA-256) of the data is computed
  3. Your private key signs the hash, creating a digital signature
  4. The signature is attached to the ticket
Signature = RSA_Sign(SHA256(TicketData), PrivateKey)
4

Encryption

For secure transmission, tickets are encrypted using hybrid encryption:

AES-256 (Symmetric)

Fast encryption of the actual ticket data

RSA-2048 (Asymmetric)

Secure key exchange for the AES key

5

Verification

Anyone can verify a ticket's authenticity:

  1. Extract the public key from the certificate
  2. Validate the certificate against the CA
  3. Compute hash of ticket data
  4. Use public key to verify the signature matches the hash
  5. Check certificate hasn't been revoked (CRL check)
Result: If verification passes, the ticket is authentic and hasn't been tampered with!

Security Features

Non-Repudiation

Only you can create signatures with your private key. You can't deny creating a ticket.

Integrity

Any modification to ticket data will invalidate the signature, detecting tampering.

Authentication

Certificates prove identity. Only the legitimate owner can sign tickets.

Confidentiality

Encryption ensures ticket data remains private during transmission.

Attack Prevention

Attack Type How PKI Prevents It
Ticket Forgery Without the private key, attackers cannot create valid signatures
Tampering Hash verification detects any changes to ticket data
Replay Attacks Timestamps and unique ticket IDs prevent reuse
Man-in-the-Middle Encryption protects data in transit; certificate validation ensures authenticity
Impersonation Digital certificates bind identity to public keys
